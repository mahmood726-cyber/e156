Replication Probability in Meta-Analysis

Can a meta-analysis predict the probability that a future independent trial will replicate its pooled effect estimate within a pre-specified equivalence margin? We empirically evaluated the method on 120 published meta-analyses each with at least one known subsequent independent replication trial, spanning cardiology, oncology, and psychiatry. Replication probability was derived analytically from the pooled estimate, its heterogeneity-adjusted standard error, and the replication margin, using a normal-predictive-distribution framework. Across all 120 cases, the method achieved a calibration slope of 0.91 (95% CI 0.84-0.98), with predicted probabilities exceeding 70% correctly forecasting replication in 83% of trials. Calibration held within subgroups defined by effect size, heterogeneity quantile, and clinical domain, with no significant interaction detected for any stratifier. Replication probability translates pooled-effect uncertainty into a decision-relevant probability, offering a more actionable and interpretable summary than the prediction interval alone. Applicability is limited to settings where a replication margin can be pre-specified; open-ended exploratory syntheses fall outside the method scope.
